The Niche Please Framework

Eleven concepts. Your whole brand inside them.

01 Niche Please · Niche Out
A different way to niche.
You stop trying to be the best version of something familiar. You start becoming the first version of something only you can lead.
Read the full pieceClose ↓

Most coaches niche in: pick a familiar label, climb the pile, claw to the top of people doing the exact same thing. That’s legibility bought with self-abandonment.

You niche out. Your work is transformational and you lead with the human, not the deliverable. One unified Category of One sits above everything, the same identity whether you’re advising a company or coaching a leader one to one. We resist splitting the brand in two.

02 Niche Please · Your Origin / The Aha
The story is the proof.

Read your storyClose ↓
The Aha Moment · The Plane
The story you tell. Present tense, in your own voice.

It’s three weeks after my last medical leave, and I’m on a plane again. Remote company. Board meetings, client meetings, always on the road. I’ve done this a thousand times.

I reach up to put my bag in the overhead bin, and I can’t. My body won’t do it. Three medical leaves, a bilateral mastectomy, fully cured, and I still can’t lift my own bag over my head. So I stand there in the aisle and I ask a stranger to lift it for me.

I don’t want to. Everything in me that built a career on being the one who carries it, who handles it, who never needs the help, is screaming. And under the screaming, lower, in my gut, something quieter: what am I doing this for?

The old voice answers fast. You’re one of the women who made it. The title, the team, the seventy-billion-dollar company. What is wrong with you. Just do it. Get through it.

But I think of my husband. Twenty years. My daughter at home. The people I got closer to in all that darkness. And I know, standing there, that I’m trying to climb back into a life that’s already gone.

That’s the moment it lands. Not in my head. In my body. The striving, the overriding, the just-get-through-it, I’d been calling that leadership my whole life. It isn’t. It never was.

I couldn’t lift the bag. So I finally set it down.

The Aha Moment · The Office
The one your body had been waiting to tell.

It’s my year-end review, and I almost prepare for it. Then I catch myself. The review with your CEO is the one meeting you don’t actually have to prepare for. So for once, I don’t. I just walk in.

It’s good. Better than good. He loves the rebrand, the numbers, all of it. We’re wrapping up, and then he stops. He looks at me and says, you know, Jen, you never really talk about how you’re doing. You’ve been through a lot these past couple of years. And then he asks the question almost nobody asks an executive. How are you. Really.

And my God, it just comes out.

I don’t decide to say it. It starts low, in my gut, and it just goes, like my body has been standing at the door for two years waiting for someone to finally open it. I’m calm. So calm. And I hear myself say it out loud for the first time: I’m not well. I’m not fully healed. Not my body, not my mind.

Because here’s what nobody tells you. After a bilateral mastectomy, it can take up to three years for a woman’s body to come back to itself. And I went back to my old life three weeks out, telling everyone I’d just travel and do all the things. And my body had been saying the whole time, quietly, the entire time: no. You’re not.

Then he asks another one. What’s a day in your life actually like. And I tell him the truth I’d never said to anyone at work. Most days I’m managing pain. If I don’t catch it early, it floods down my whole body. Some days I’m on video calls with the camera off because I’m lying on the floor trying to get comfortable. Some days it’s ice packs I don’t want anyone to see. Some days it’s the anxiety I never used to have, and the guilt of being away from my family, all of it tangled up with how much I still love this work and these people.

And underneath all of it, the old voice, the loud one: you’re one of the women who made it. You have everything. What is wrong with you. Just do it. Get through it.

But I can’t unhear what just happened. The second he asked, and actually waited for the answer, my body told the truth my mind hadn’t caught up to yet. I don’t survive this and go back to what normal was. That woman isn’t coming back. And sitting in that office, for the first time, I realize I don’t want her to.

My mind had been running the company. My body had been keeping the record. All it needed was for one person to ask, and mean it.

The through-line of your whole life

You spent twenty-five-plus years in healthcare, most of it climbing, and rose to Chief Marketing and Communications Officer of a seventy-billion-dollar company. By every external measure, one of the women who made it.

Then you got breast cancer. You ran enterprise marketing through chemotherapy, a bilateral mastectomy, and three medical leaves. You’re now fully cured. And somewhere in the middle of returning to “normal,” your body told you the truth your mind had been managing around: you could not go back to the life you left.

There’s a moment you return to. Three weeks out from your last leave, on a plane for work, you couldn’t lift your own bag into the overhead bin. You had to ask a stranger for help. That was the moment the question got loud: what am I doing this for?

So you left. About a year ago you became, in your words, a free bird. You started newmorrow. You now work with a small, deliberate portfolio of leaders and founders, building the thing you wish you’d had when you were the one who couldn’t lift your own bag.

In twenty-six years, almost every role you held was one you had to define yourself. You’ve always lived in the undefined. You’re also a self-described Woo girl: manifestation, human design, Reiki, acupuncture, sound baths. Through cancer you built a care team of Eastern and Western practitioners who would talk to each other. You don’t see two worlds. You see yourself as the bridge.

03 Niche Please · The Point
What you stand for, and against.
The world needs more strong leaders, not fewer, but not at the cost of themselves. The enemy isn’t ambition. It’s the unsustainable model.
See the for / againstClose ↓
What you’re for
  • More leadership, not less. The goal is never to talk anyone out of leading.
  • A ladder worth climbing. Not opting out of ambition, redefining the destination.
  • Impact without self-abandonment. Big, bold, successful, and taking care of yourself.
  • Sustainable, meaningful, aligned with the life you actually want to live.
What you’re against
  • The override. Success built on overriding your health, relationships, and self. You paid that bill.
  • “We’re a family” culture theater. Honest culture, not performed belonging.
  • Lipstick on a pig marketing. A logo change isn’t a strategy.
  • Niching into a transactional box. You lead with the human, not the deliverable.

06 Niche Please · The Signature Chord
Three ordinary notes nobody else plays together.
You don’t become a category of one by being weird on any single note. You become one by playing three at once that nobody else combines: your Way, your What, your We.

The Way · how you work
Trusted advisor & healer in one

You lead with the human first, let the strategy follow. You read energy and keep the freedom to say no. Cloud-moving: you set the vision and orchestrate the partners who execute it. You sit in the hard truth, then move them to neutral.

Depleted-and-striving → whole-and-deliberate

Not the deliverable. The becoming. A leader who has real impact without overriding her wellbeing, designs life on her own terms, and knows who she is on the other side of everything she built.

The hardest, highest-leverage note. Right now you have an audience and a network, not yet a named room. The build is a movement of women building a ladder worth climbing. Candidates to test: “I’m a New Morrow leader.” “Whole-life leaders.”

You’re a trusted-advisor-and-healer, who takes leaders from depleted-and-striving to whole-and-deliberate, inside a movement of women building a ladder worth climbing. No one else plays those three notes at once.
07 Niche Please · The We / The Room
Who you’re for. And who you’re not.
You’re polarizing on purpose, and that’s an asset. The clearer you are about who you’re for, the faster the right people lean in and the wrong people leave.
See your roomClose ↓
You’re for her if
  • She’s a leader at an inflection point, stepping into the biggest role of her career, or quietly outgrowing the one she’s in.
  • She’s a high achiever figuring out who she is on the other side of everything she’s built.
  • She wants the human truth underneath her message, not a fresh coat of paint.
  • She wants impact and a life, and she’s done pretending those are mutually exclusive.
  • She’s at least curious about the unseen. Not a woo girl, but not allergic to it.
You’re not for her if
  • She wants someone to make her climb faster up a ladder leaning on the wrong wall.
  • She wants a vendor or a vending machine. You’re a value match, not a service desk.
  • She wants the logo without the purpose underneath it.

08 Niche Please · Holding the Edge
The woo, as a bridge.
The move isn’t to hide it, and isn’t to lead with chakras. The move is to make you the bridge, so the woo becomes the most valuable thing you offer, not the thing that scares anyone off.
How to hold itClose ↓

You bring the unseen into the boardroom in language executives already trust.

Energy becomes “what the room is actually telling you.” Alignment becomes “the decision your body already made.” Intuition becomes “the data you have that isn’t in the deck.”

You’re the proof that both can be true.

Eastern and Western practitioners on the same care team got you through cancer. You run your business the same way, hard strategy and soul, together.

You’re grounded, relatable, and in the 3D.

That’s precisely what lets you carry people who’d never follow a more out-there teacher. Accessibility is the gift.

09 Niche Please · The Voice
So the copy reads like you.
Grounded executive plus soul. Warm but not soft, direct, a little irreverent, declarative. You have, in your words, “rich resting bitch face,” and your writing carries that same calm, knowing authority.
Do’s, don’ts & your phrasesClose ↓

Voice do’s

  • Lead with the human and the story, then land the point.
  • Be declarative. Short, certain sentences.
  • Use your real phrases (below).
  • Let warmth and edge coexist. “This isn’t a fit” and “I adore you” in one breath.
  • Keep the woo grounded. Translate, don’t mystify.

Voice don’ts

  • No corporate filler. No “leverage,” “unlock,” “elevate,” “synergy.”
  • No guru-speak. The accessible bridge, not the high priestess.
  • No false humility, no hard hustle. Neither shrinking nor grinding.
  • Don’t bury the story under frameworks. The story is the proof.
